# First Nations Protocol
## Frankston 2035 — Chunk 06

*A protocol for listening, relationship, permission, and process.*
*This document does not speak for any First Nations community.*
*It describes how Blue Snake Studio will conduct itself.*

---

## Part 1 — Preamble: Why This Protocol Exists

Public art projects are not culturally neutral.

A mural on a wall in Frankston is on Country. A walk through the foreshore is on Country. A program in a school or library is on Country. The Bunurong people of the Kulin Nation are the Traditional Custodians of this land and its waters. Their sovereignty has never been ceded.

This protocol exists because good intentions without good process cause harm.

Blue Snake Studio does not begin from a position of existing relationship with the Bunurong community or any other First Nations community in the Frankston area. This is not something to be embarrassed about. It is something to be honest about.

We start where we are: outside. We proceed slowly, by invitation, and only as far as we are welcomed.

This protocol governs everything.

---

## Part 2 — What We Do Not Claim

Blue Snake Studio does not:

- Claim to represent, speak for, or act on behalf of any First Nations community
- Claim knowledge of, access to, or permission to use First Nations language, stories, symbols, or cultural material
- Claim existing relationships that do not exist
- Assume that any First Nations individual, organisation, or elder can speak for all First Nations people
- Assume that Acknowledgement of Country constitutes meaningful engagement
- Assume that this protocol, however carefully written, is sufficient without genuine relationship

No document, however well-intentioned, substitutes for trust built over time.

---

## Part 3 — Relationship Before Project

**The sequence matters.**

```
Relationship → Trust → Conversation → Permission → Collaboration
```

We do not reverse this sequence. We do not skip steps.

A project cannot begin — regardless of funding, timeline, or community enthusiasm — if the relevant relationships have not been established at the pace the relevant community sets.

If a funder requires First Nations engagement faster than relationship allows, we decline the condition or the funding. We do not manufacture relationships.

**Who initiates contact:**
The Creative Director approaches through existing, trusted intermediaries where possible — arts organisations, health and cultural services, community networks — not cold outreach. First contact is a request for a conversation, not a project proposal.

**What the first conversation is:**
A listening session. We share who we are and what we are trying to do. We listen to what is important, what is sensitive, what is unwelcome. We do not bring a brief, a timeline, or a budget.

---

## Part 4 — The Listening Posture

When in conversation with First Nations individuals or organisations, Blue Snake Studio operates in listening mode.

**We bring:**
- A genuine account of the project and its purpose
- Honest acknowledgement of what we do not know
- Flexibility about scope, timing, and involvement
- Respect for the time given to us

**We do not bring:**
- A pre-formed creative direction seeking affirmation
- A brief that assumes cultural content will be provided
- A timeline driven by external funding deadlines
- An expectation of reciprocity within a meeting

**We do not record** conversations, meetings, or consultations without explicit, informed consent. Notes are shared back for accuracy.

**We do not share** the content of conversations with any third party — including Council, funders, or other partners — without explicit permission.

---

## Part 5 — Seeking Permission

**Permission is specific, not general.**

Permission to engage in conversation is not permission to use cultural material.
Permission to collaborate on one project is not permission to collaborate on the next.
Permission from one community member is not permission from the community.

**The permission process:**

1. Clearly describe what is being asked
2. Describe how the material or contribution will be used
3. Describe who will see it, where it will appear, how long it will remain
4. Describe how the contributor will be credited
5. Describe who owns the outcome
6. Allow adequate time for consideration — weeks, not hours
7. Document the permission in writing, countersigned
8. Revisit permission if the scope changes

**Payment:**
Cultural advice, participation, and contribution are professional services. They are compensated at professional rates. Asking First Nations people to contribute their knowledge, time, or cultural expertise without payment is exploitation, regardless of intent.

---

## Part 6 — When Permission Is Not Granted

**No means no. It does not mean try again differently.**

If a community, organisation, or individual declines to participate, declines to provide permission, or asks the project to stay away from certain material, places, or ideas:

- We document the decision
- We remove the element from scope
- We do not ask a different person the same question hoping for a different answer
- We do not interpret partial engagement as implicit permission

If permission is withdrawn after it has been given:
- We stop immediately
- We work with the community to remove or modify affected material
- We bear the cost of the change

There is no creative direction, no funding condition, and no public commitment that overrides a withdrawal of permission.

---

## Part 7 — Cultural Safety in Public Spaces

Public artworks exist in shared, unsupervised spaces. This creates specific obligations.

**Before any publicly visible artwork is finalised:**

- Check whether the location, imagery, or content has any cultural sensitivity
- Check whether the work could be read as misrepresenting, appropriating, or trivialising First Nations culture
- Get external review from a qualified cultural safety advisor
- This check applies even to work that does not appear to involve First Nations subject matter

**Ongoing:**

If a community member raises a concern about a completed artwork — regardless of when the concern is raised — the Creative Director responds within 48 hours and initiates a review. The review process is transparent and documented.

---

## Part 8 — When Projects Touch Country, Story, or Site

Some projects will, by their nature, intersect with Country, cultural sites, or stories — even when that is not their primary purpose.

**The QR Art Walk** moves through Frankston's public spaces. Some of those spaces carry meaning, history, or cultural significance that is not visible in the landscape.

**Living Murals** in schools may be near, or visible from, places that carry significance.

**The Protocol:**

Before any project is installed or activated, the Creative Director:

1. Researches whether the site or its surrounds carries documented cultural significance (through public records, council heritage registers, and Registered Aboriginal Parties maps)
2. Brings findings to any existing First Nations advisors on the project
3. If significance is identified: does not proceed without explicit permission from the relevant community
4. If significance is uncertain: treats it as significant until clarified
5. Documents the process regardless of outcome

**Registered Aboriginal Party:**
The Bunurong Land Council Aboriginal Corporation is the Registered Aboriginal Party (RAP) for the Frankston area. Any project touching cultural heritage or Country should engage with the RAP through their proper process, not through informal channels.

This protocol does not replace the RAP process. It precedes it.

---

## Part 9 — Accountability and Review

**This protocol is not self-executing.**

A written protocol without accountability is a piece of paper.

**Internal accountability:**

- The Creative Director is personally responsible for following this protocol
- Every decision made under this protocol is documented in the project's records
- Breaches of this protocol are reported transparently — to Council partners, funders, and the community — not managed quietly

**External accountability:**

- An independent cultural safety advisor will be engaged (on their terms) to review this protocol before it is used operationally
- That advisor's feedback will be incorporated into v2.0
- The protocol is reviewed annually

**What we will not do:**

We will not use this protocol as a shield — citing its existence as evidence of cultural safety without doing the actual work.

The quality of our First Nations relationships is not measured by this document. It is measured by whether First Nations people in Frankston, over time, describe Blue Snake Studio as an organisation that kept its word.

That is the only measure that matters.
