# First Nations Protocol
## Frankston 2035 — Chunk 06 — Full Treatment

*A protocol for listening, relationship, permission, and process.*

*This document does not speak for any First Nations community.*
*It describes how Blue Snake Studio will conduct itself.*
*It has not yet been reviewed by any First Nations community or advisor.*
*It must not be used operationally until that review has occurred.*

---

## Part 1 — Preamble: Why This Protocol Exists

Public art projects are not culturally neutral.

A mural on a school wall in Frankston is on Country. A walk through the foreshore is on Country. A program in a library, a song recorded in a studio, a QR code embedded in a footpath — all of it is on Country. The Bunurong people of the Kulin Nation are the Traditional Custodians of this land and its waters. Their sovereignty has never been ceded.

This is not a formality. It is a fact with consequences.

For Blue Snake Studio, those consequences are practical: every project this organisation runs in Frankston sits within a landscape of living cultural significance, whether or not that significance is visible to us. We may not see it. We may not know it is there. That does not make it absent.

This protocol exists because good intentions without good process cause harm. The history of arts and cultural organisations working alongside First Nations communities in Australia is not uniformly good. It includes genuine partnership and profound disrespect, often from the same organisations in different decades. The disrespect has usually not come from malice. It has come from speed, assumption, and the belief that a good outcome justifies a compromised process.

It does not.

Blue Snake Studio does not begin this project from a position of existing relationship with the Bunurong community or any other First Nations community in the Frankston area. We state this plainly. It is not something to conceal or to dress up. It is the honest starting point, and honesty is the only foundation a relationship can be built on.

We start where we are: outside. We proceed slowly, by invitation, and only as far as we are welcomed.

This protocol is not a checklist to complete. It is a set of commitments about how we behave, held to account by internal records and external review. It governs everything that follows.

**What this protocol does not do:**

It does not substitute for relationship. It does not constitute engagement. It does not prove cultural safety. It does not make Blue Snake Studio an organisation that First Nations people should trust — only our actions, over time, can do that.

This document is the starting position. The ending position will be determined by the communities we work alongside.

## Part 2 — What We Do Not Claim

This section exists because arts organisations routinely claim things they have not earned, often without realising they are doing it.

We name the claims we do not make so that we can catch ourselves before we make them.

---

**We do not claim to represent, speak for, or act on behalf of any First Nations community.**

This means: we do not write grant applications in which First Nations voices are quoted or implied without explicit, individual consent. We do not describe our project as "First Nations-inclusive" because we have attended one meeting. We do not use language like "in partnership with First Nations communities" unless a formal, documented, consented partnership exists.

**We do not claim knowledge of, access to, or permission to use First Nations language, stories, symbols, or cultural material.**

This means: even if a cultural element feels generic, universal, or decorative — it is not ours to use. The belief that certain First Nations imagery has passed into common ownership is a misunderstanding. No element of First Nations cultural expression is available for use without permission from the relevant community.

**We do not claim existing relationships that do not exist.**

This means: if a funder asks whether we have community support, we say the truth: we are building towards it. We do not cite a meeting as a relationship, or a relationship as a partnership, or a partnership as a permission.

**We do not assume that any First Nations individual, organisation, or elder can speak for all First Nations people.**

The Bunurong community is not a monolith. First Nations Australia is not a monolith. Permission from one person does not constitute permission from a community. Support from one organisation does not constitute cultural clearance. We do not flatten complexity for the sake of convenience.

**We do not assume that Acknowledgement of Country constitutes meaningful engagement.**

Acknowledgement of Country is appropriate and necessary. It is not engagement. It is not consultation. It is not permission. It does not replace any step in this protocol. Treating Acknowledgement as sufficient is one of the most common failures in this space, and it fails precisely because it feels respectful while functioning as avoidance.

**We do not assume that this protocol, however carefully written, is sufficient without genuine relationship.**

A well-written protocol is a commitment to behave a certain way. It is the floor, not the ceiling. The floor can be crossed; the ceiling must be built through time, trust, and demonstrated consistency.

**We do not assume that urgency excuses process.**

Funding deadlines, Council timelines, community enthusiasm — none of these create permission to compress the relationship-building that this protocol requires. An organisation that is in a genuine hurry should either slow down or acknowledge that it is not ready for this kind of engagement yet.

## Part 3 — Relationship Before Project

The sequence matters. This is the foundational principle of this entire protocol.

```
RESEARCH → INTERMEDIARY → FIRST CONTACT → LISTENING → TIME
    ↓                                                    ↓
DOCUMENT                                          RELATIONSHIP
    ↓                                                    ↓
                                              (only then) PROJECT
```

The sequence cannot be reversed. It cannot be compressed. It cannot be faked.

---

### What "Relationship" Means Here

Relationship, in this context, is not friendship. It is not professional familiarity. It is the kind of connection in which both parties can be honest with each other — including honest about disagreement — and both parties feel that the connection is worth maintaining.

That kind of relationship takes years in most human contexts. It takes longer here, because the context includes a history of broken trust between institutions and First Nations communities that Blue Snake Studio did not create but benefits from and must actively work against.

We do not set a timeline for relationship. The relationship develops at the pace the community sets.

### The Research Phase

Before any contact is made, the Creative Director:

1. Researches the history of the area — not to become an expert, but to understand the landscape before walking into it
2. Reviews publicly available information about the Bunurong Land Council Aboriginal Corporation and other First Nations organisations active in the Frankston area
3. Identifies any arts organisations, health services, or cultural programmes already operating relationships with First Nations communities in the area
4. Identifies potential intermediaries — people or organisations who can make a respectful introduction where appropriate
5. Notes anything in the project scope that may be particularly sensitive

This research is documented. It shapes how we approach contact, not whether we approach it.

### Finding Intermediaries

Cold contact — reaching out to a First Nations organisation or individual with no prior introduction — is not prohibited, but it is not the preferred approach. An introduction through a trusted shared connection creates a different quality of first conversation.

Potential intermediaries include:
- Arts organisations with established First Nations programs (Regional Arts Victoria, local arts centres)
- Health and community services working with First Nations communities in Frankston
- Schools with existing First Nations support programs
- Council's own cultural heritage and community services staff

We ask intermediaries only to make an introduction. We do not ask them to advocate for the project, provide endorsement, or negotiate on our behalf.

### First Contact

First contact is a request for a conversation — not a project pitch.

It contains:
- Who we are, in plain language
- What Frankston 2035 is, in plain language
- An honest statement that we are at the beginning of this process
- A request for a conversation at a time and place of their choosing
- No attached brief, no budget, no timeline

We do not follow up a first contact more than once. If we do not hear back, we accept that as information about readiness and timing, not as a problem to be solved.

### The First Conversation

The first conversation is a listening session. We are not presenting. We are not seeking approval. We are not establishing whether this community will be "involved" in our project.

We bring:
- A genuine account of what we are trying to do and why
- Honest acknowledgement of what we do not know
- An open posture about what involvement, if any, might look like
- Enough time to sit with silence if that is what the conversation produces

We leave with whatever was given to us — no more.

### Patience as Practice

Patience in this context is not passive waiting. It is active respect.

It means continuing to build the project in ways that do not create pressure on the relationship. It means showing up consistently, without urgency. It means returning when invited, not when it is convenient for the project's timeline.

If the relationship is not yet established when a pilot launch date approaches, the launch date moves — or the element that requires this relationship is removed from scope.

## Part 4 — The Listening Posture

When in conversation with First Nations individuals or organisations, Blue Snake Studio operates in listening mode. This is a specific, active posture — not simply the absence of talking.

---

### What Listening Looks Like in Practice

**Before the conversation:**
- Review any prior notes from previous conversations. Consistency matters.
- Come with no agenda beyond being present.
- Turn off recording devices. Ask again at the start of the meeting whether notes are acceptable. If not: no notes.
- Arrive on time. Leave when the conversation ends, not when the agenda is complete.

**During the conversation:**
- Ask open questions. Listen to the full answer before the next question.
- Do not redirect toward project-relevant topics. Follow where the conversation goes.
- If something sensitive is shared: receive it, do not probe it, do not repeat it.
- If you do not understand something: say so simply. Do not pretend to understand.
- Do not fill silence. Silence is part of the conversation.
- Do not make commitments in the moment. Say: "I will need to think about that."

**After the conversation:**
- Write up notes within 24 hours, from memory, not from a recording.
- Share the notes back to the person or organisation for accuracy check.
- Follow through on anything you said you would do.
- Do not share the content of the conversation with any third party — including Council, funders, or project partners — without explicit permission.
- Record the conversation in the project's internal log (date, participants, general topics only — no specifics that were shared in confidence).

### The Difference Between Listening and Research

Research extracts information for a purpose. Listening receives what is offered without a purpose in mind.

If the Creative Director enters a conversation with a list of things they need to know, they are not listening — they are researching. Research without consent, in this context, is a form of extraction.

If something shared in a conversation becomes useful to the project later, permission must be sought specifically to use it — even if it was shared freely.

### Testing Whether You Have Listened

A useful question to ask yourself after any conversation: could I say something back to this person that would make them feel understood?

Not agreed with. Not necessarily going to change our project. But understood.

If the answer is no — you were not listening. Go back.

## Part 5 — Seeking Permission

Permission is the fulcrum of this entire protocol.

Everything before Part 5 is preparation for being worthy of asking. Everything after Part 5 depends on what the answer is.

---

### Permission Is Specific, Not General

These distinctions are non-negotiable:

- Permission to have a conversation ≠ permission to use cultural material
- Permission to collaborate on this project ≠ permission to collaborate on the next
- Permission from one individual ≠ permission from their community
- Permission for this use ≠ permission for a different use of the same material
- Permission given now ≠ permission that cannot be withdrawn later
- Verbal permission ≠ written permission (for anything that will be publicly used)

Every one of these distinctions has been collapsed by arts organisations in Australia, with harmful results. We hold them open.

### Types of Permission

**Verbal permission** is valid for:
- Having a conversation
- Attending an event or community gathering
- Informal collaboration at an early exploratory stage

**Written permission** is required for:
- Using any cultural material, symbol, language, or story in a project
- Crediting a First Nations individual or organisation in public materials
- Including any record of a conversation or consultation in public documents
- Ongoing collaboration of any kind

**What "written permission" means:**
A document that describes:
1. Who is giving permission and in what capacity
2. What specifically is permitted
3. How it will be used (medium, audience, duration, location)
4. How the contributor will be credited
5. Who owns the outcome
6. The right to withdraw permission, and on what terms
7. Any conditions or limitations
8. Signatures of both parties
9. Date

This is not a legal contract in the first instance — it is a record of an honest conversation made permanent. Legal formalisation may follow for significant or long-term arrangements.

### The Permission Process

1. **Describe what you are asking for.** Be specific. Not "we'd love your involvement" — "we are asking whether you are willing to [specific thing] for [specific purpose] in [specific timeframe]."

2. **Describe how it will be used.** Who will see it. Where it will appear. Whether it is temporary or permanent. Whether it is commercial or non-commercial. Whether it can be reproduced.

3. **Describe attribution.** How will the contributor be credited? Where? In what form? What if the attribution cannot be given (e.g. in a context where the format doesn't allow it)?

4. **Describe ownership.** Who owns the outcome? If it is Blue Snake Studio or the community institution, is there any ongoing right for the contributor?

5. **Allow time.** Weeks, not days. Not "by end of week" or "before the deadline." We say: "There is no hurry. Please take whatever time you need."

6. **Receive the answer without negotiating it.** If the answer is partial, accept the partial permission and work within it. Do not counter-offer.

7. **Document in writing.** Even verbal permissions that are not for public use are noted in the project log.

8. **Revisit if scope changes.** If the project changes — new audience, different medium, extended duration — permission must be sought again. Scope change without fresh permission is a breach of this protocol.

### Payment

Cultural knowledge, advice, time, and contribution are professional services. They are compensated at professional rates.

- Consultation: AUD $150–250/hour (minimum), or market rate if higher
- Collaboration credit and fee: negotiated with the contributor, not by BSS unilaterally
- No "in-kind" arrangement substitutes for payment unless explicitly requested by the contributor

Asking First Nations people to contribute their expertise for free — even in the name of a good cause — is exploitation. The cause does not change the dynamic.

## Part 6 — When Permission Is Not Granted

No means no.

It does not mean: try a different framing.
It does not mean: find a different person who might say yes.
It does not mean: proceed with slightly less of the thing you wanted.
It does not mean: wait for the deadline to create pressure.

---

### How to Receive a Refusal

When permission is not granted, the Creative Director:

1. Thanks the person or organisation for their consideration
2. Does not ask for reasons
3. Does not express disappointment in a way that creates pressure
4. Does not suggest alternatives in the same conversation
5. Confirms that the decision is understood and will be respected

A refusal is information. It is not a problem to be solved.

### What to Do After a Refusal

- Document the decision: who, what was asked, what was declined, date
- Remove the element from project scope immediately
- Do not resurface it in a later conversation unless the community raises it
- Do not ask a different community member or organisation the same question

If a funder or partner asks why an element has been removed from scope, the response is: "We sought permission and it was not granted. We respect that decision." Nothing more.

We do not share the identity of who declined or the reasoning they offered — even to our Council partners or funders. That information belongs to the community, not to us.

### When Permission Is Withdrawn

Permission, once given, can be withdrawn at any time.

If permission is withdrawn:
- We stop using the material or collaboration immediately
- We remove or modify any public-facing element as rapidly as possible
- We bear all costs of the change
- We do not negotiate the withdrawal
- We document the decision and the outcome

There is no creative direction, no published work, no funding commitment, and no public announcement that overrides a withdrawal of permission. If we have made a public commitment that cannot be met because permission has been withdrawn, we acknowledge this publicly without identifying the community's decision.

### The Emotional Reality

Receiving a refusal — particularly after months of relationship-building — is genuinely difficult. This protocol acknowledges that.

The difficulty is information too. It may indicate that the project was moving faster than the relationship could support. It may indicate that an element of the project was less welcome than expected. It may indicate nothing about us at all — communities are complex and have their own priorities.

We do not process this difficulty with the community. We process it internally, or with an external advisor. We return to the community, when appropriate, with the same openness we brought the first time.

## Part 7 — Cultural Safety in Public Spaces

Public artworks are different from private projects. A mural on a school wall cannot be unseen. A QR code on a footpath will be scanned by people who know nothing about the project's intentions. A permanent installation outlasts its creators.

This creates a specific obligation: cultural safety must be designed in, not added as a review at the end.

---

### What Cultural Safety Means in This Context

Cultural safety, in public art, is the condition in which:
- First Nations people who encounter the work are not diminished, misrepresented, or disrespected by it
- Cultural material is not used without appropriate permission
- The work does not reinforce harmful stereotypes or historical distortions
- First Nations community members in the area feel the work acknowledges their presence correctly

Cultural safety is not the same as cultural avoidance. A project can acknowledge First Nations cultural presence without using cultural material — through appropriate Acknowledgement, through visible absence (a wall left blank where permission was not given), through process transparency.

### The Cultural Safety Audit

Before any publicly visible artwork or installation is finalised, the Creative Director commissions an independent cultural safety review.

A qualified cultural safety advisor:
- Is an Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander person with professional experience in cultural safety in arts or community contexts
- Is not affiliated with Blue Snake Studio or any project partner
- Is paid at professional rates for their review
- Provides a written report with recommendations
- Is not asked to simply approve the work — they are asked to identify anything that may cause harm

The audit covers:
- The site location (any cultural significance?)
- The visual content (any inadvertent misrepresentation or appropriation?)
- The narrative content (any historical inaccuracy or harmful framing?)
- The community consultation process (was it adequate?)
- The permission documentation (is it complete?)

The audit report becomes part of the project's permanent record.

### Ongoing Cultural Safety

A completed artwork requires ongoing cultural safety monitoring.

- If a community member — First Nations or otherwise — raises a concern about a completed artwork, the Creative Director responds within 48 hours
- The concern is documented
- A review is initiated
- The outcome of the review is transparent and documented
- If the concern identifies a genuine issue, the response is rapid and the cost is borne by BSS

There is no statute of limitations. A mural from 2027 can be questioned in 2033 and the protocol applies in full.

### Cultural Safety Is Not Cultural Avoidance

Avoiding all reference to First Nations people, history, and culture in public art is not cultural safety — it is erasure. Frankston is on Country. That fact can and should be present in the project's work, through appropriate Acknowledgement, through invitations to community participation, and through genuine, permitted engagement when that engagement has been established.

The goal is not a project scrubbed clean of First Nations reference. The goal is a project in which every reference is made with care, permission, and honesty.

## Part 8 — When Projects Touch Country, Story, or Site

Some projects will intersect with Country, cultural sites, or stories — even when that is not their stated purpose.

The QR Art Walk moves through Frankston's public spaces. The Living Mural is installed in a school on Country. The Library program occupies a building on Country. None of these projects are "about" First Nations culture — but all of them exist within it.

---

### What "Country" Means

We use the word "Country" as it is used in Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Australian contexts: not simply geographic land, but a living relationship between people, place, waterways, sky, animals, plants, and story. Country carries meaning that is not always recorded in public heritage registers. Significance can be present in places that appear, to a non-Aboriginal eye, to be unremarkable.

We do not claim to understand Country. We acknowledge that it exists, that it carries meaning we may not be able to see, and that this requires us to proceed with care.

### Researching Cultural Significance

Before any project is installed or activated, the Creative Director:

1. Reviews publicly available heritage information: Victorian Heritage Register, Council heritage registers, Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Management Plans if any exist for the area
2. Checks the Aboriginal Victoria Registered Aboriginal Parties map to confirm the Bunurong Land Council Aboriginal Corporation as the relevant RAP for the site
3. Notes any sites, paths, waterways, or landforms that may carry significance that is not in public records
4. Brings this research to any existing First Nations advisors on the project before proceeding

**If significance is identified:** Does not proceed without explicit permission from the relevant community or RAP.

**If significance is uncertain:** Treats it as significant until clarified.

**If public records are silent:** Does not interpret silence as absence of significance.

**Documents the research process regardless of outcome.**

### The Registered Aboriginal Party Process

The Bunurong Land Council Aboriginal Corporation is the Registered Aboriginal Party (RAP) for the Frankston area under the Aboriginal Heritage Act 2006 (Vic).

The RAP process is a formal legal mechanism for engaging with cultural heritage. It is not a substitute for relationship — it is a separate, parallel process with its own requirements.

Relevant requirements under the Aboriginal Heritage Act 2006 (Vic):
- Cultural Heritage Management Plans (CHMPs) may be required for certain activities
- Cultural Heritage Permits may be required for others
- Consultation with the RAP is required in specified circumstances

Blue Snake Studio will engage with the RAP process through its proper channels — not informally, not through intermediaries, not as a substitute for genuine relationship-building.

**This protocol does not replace the RAP process. It precedes it.**

Understanding the distinction:
- RAP process: legal, formal, procedural
- This protocol: relational, ethical, ongoing

Both are required. Neither substitutes for the other.

### Pilot-Specific Obligations

**Pilot 01 — Living Mural:**
Before any school wall is selected, the cultural heritage of the site and its surrounds is researched. Before any mural concept is approved, a cultural safety audit is commissioned. The mural's content does not incorporate any cultural imagery without explicit, documented permission.

**Pilot 02 — Healthy Tech Library:**
The Library building's cultural landscape is acknowledged. The program acknowledges Country in all public-facing materials. No digital content touches First Nations cultural material without permission.

**Pilot 03 — QR Art Walk:**
Every stop on the walk is site-checked for cultural significance before QR content is developed. Any stop near, at, or referencing a site of significance follows the full permission pathway. No audio, visual, or textual content references First Nations culture without explicit, documented permission. An Acknowledgement of Country appears at the first stop and in the printed walk map.

## Part 9 — Accountability and Review

This protocol is not self-executing. Writing it is the beginning of the work, not the end.

---

### Internal Accountability

**The Creative Director is personally responsible** for following this protocol. Not a staff member. Not a consultant. The Creative Director.

Every decision made under this protocol is documented in the project's internal log:
- Date
- Context
- Decision made
- Who was consulted
- What was documented

The log is not a defensive record — it is a learning record. If we look back on a decision and see that we could have done better, we document that too.

**Breaches of this protocol are reported transparently.**

A breach is any action that bypasses or compromises a part of this protocol. Breaches are not managed quietly. They are reported to all project partners — Council, funders, community partners — and a corrective action is documented.

The impulse to manage a breach quietly is understandable. We resist it, because the pattern of managing breaches quietly is one of the primary ways institutions lose trust.

### External Accountability

**Phase 1 — Before operational use:**
An independent cultural safety advisor, engaged on their terms, reviews this protocol and provides written feedback. Their feedback is incorporated into v2.0. This review is a prerequisite for any First Nations engagement.

**Phase 2 — Annual review:**
This protocol is reviewed annually against:
- What happened in the preceding twelve months
- Whether the protocol was followed
- What worked and what didn't
- Any changes in the legal or community landscape
- Feedback from any First Nations individuals or organisations we have had contact with (with their consent to share it)

The review is conducted by the Creative Director and, where possible, by an external reviewer who is not affiliated with the project.

**Phase 3 — Relationship review:**
At the end of each pilot, we seek feedback — if a relationship has been established — on how Blue Snake Studio behaved. Not on the creative outcome. On the process.

We will not always receive feedback. We always ask.

### What a Breach Looks Like

A breach of this protocol is any instance in which:
- Cultural material is used without documented permission
- A relationship is claimed that does not exist
- The permission sequence is bypassed or compressed
- A refusal is ignored or worked around
- Confidential information shared in a meeting is disclosed without permission
- A community member's time is used without compensation
- Any part of this protocol is overridden by a deadline, a funder requirement, or a creative preference

Breaches are documented, reported, and corrected. There are no exceptions.

### The Long-Term Vision

This protocol is a beginning, not an endpoint.

The ideal outcome — over years, not months — is that Blue Snake Studio has genuine, long-term relationships with First Nations communities in Frankston based on demonstrated trustworthiness. In that future, this protocol is less necessary because the relationship itself carries the knowledge of how to proceed.

We are not there yet. We may not reach it. The direction matters regardless.

### The Only Measurement That Matters

The quality of this work is not measured by:
- Whether we have a protocol
- Whether we have an Acknowledgement of Country
- Whether we have attended community events
- Whether our grant applications mention First Nations engagement

It is measured by this: over time, do First Nations people in Frankston describe Blue Snake Studio as an organisation that kept its word?

That is the only measurement.

Everything else in this document is in service of that one outcome.
