# Council Deck Scaffold
## Frankston 2035 — 12 Slides

*A pitch scaffold, not a finished presentation.*
*Each slide has one message. If a slide has two messages, cut one.*
*Bring this to a 45-minute Council conversation. Leave it behind.*

---

## Slide 01 — Title

**Headline:** Frankston 2035 — Art That Lives

**Visual:** Hero Diagram (full, from HERO-DIAGRAM.md)

**Spoken opening:** "We're going to show you a system, not a project. A system that Frankston can own, that grows over ten years, and that starts with three small pilots. Here's what it looks like."

**What this slide does:** Establishes the frame immediately. The diagram does the work. Don't over-explain.

---

## Slide 02 — The Problem

**Headline:** Frankston's creativity is invisible.

**Content (three statements, each one line):**
- Young people in Frankston have fewer publicly funded creative opportunities than comparable Victorian cities.
- Technology in schools and libraries increasingly extracts children's data and attention.
- Public art exists — but it does not teach, it does not connect, and it does not grow.

**Visual:** Single large quotation or a simple three-item list. No charts.

**Spoken line:** "Frankston has creative people. What it lacks is creative infrastructure — the systems that connect individual acts of creativity to civic life."

**What this slide does:** Names the gap without being negative about Frankston. The problem is structural, not cultural.

**Next step visible:** "Infrastructure is the answer."

## Slide 04 — Core Idea: Creativity as Infrastructure

**Headline:** Art is infrastructure — not event, not decoration.

**Content:** One sentence per principle.

- A mural can teach. A library can create. A walk can tell a city's story.
- Creative infrastructure lasts beyond the project, the funding cycle, and the people who built it.
- It belongs to the community — not to an agency, not to a Council term.

**Visual:** The five-layer infrastructure diagram (from 07b-visual-system-pack.md, Section A1). Simple, horizontal bands.

**Spoken line:** "The difference between a program and infrastructure is whether it's still running when we're not in the room. That's what we're building."

**What this slide does:** Reframes creativity from "nice to have" to "civic essential."

**Next step visible:** "Here's how it works."

---

## Slide 05 — How the System Works

**Headline:** One system. Three settings. Every Frankston resident.

**Visual:** Hero Diagram (reduced version — postcard format, centred on slide).

**Content (below or beside diagram):**
- Investment flows from Frankston into infrastructure (schools, libraries, public art)
- Infrastructure delivers programs (murals, QR walks, healthy tech, learning packs)
- Programs reach people (children, families, artists)
- People become creators
- Creators strengthen Frankston
- Frankston reinvests — the cycle continues

**Spoken line:** "Every element feeds every other element. When a child learns from a mural, the school relationship deepens. When an artist is commissioned, the community sees its creative identity reflected back."

**What this slide does:** Shows that this is a system, not a series of disconnected projects.

**Next step visible:** "Here are the three pilots that prove it."

## Slide 07 — Recommended First Pilot

**Headline:** Start with the Library Creative Pack. Lowest risk. Fastest proof.

**Content:**

**What it is:** A curated set of offline creative tools — drawing, music, writing, building — installed in Frankston Library. No accounts. No tracking. No advertising.

**Why it's first:**
- Single partnership (Library Service)
- No software development risk
- No First Nations cultural content requirements
- Visible, permanent outcome in 3–4 months
- Builds the Library relationship needed for Pilot 02
- Demonstrates Healthy Technology in practice — Council can see and point to it

**Cost:** $20,000–$30,000
**Council contribution sought:** $12,000–$15,000
**BSS contribution / grant-matched:** Balance

**Visual:** Simple 2-column layout. Left: what it is. Right: why it's first.

**Spoken line:** "We're not asking Council to bet everything on a ten-year vision. We're asking you to co-invest in one shelf of tools in a library. If it works — and it will — that's the proof we need for everything else."

**What this slide does:** Converts the systemic pitch into a specific, low-risk, affordable commitment.

**Next step visible:** "What's already protecting children in these programs."

## Slide 08 — Healthy Technology Safeguards

**Headline:** No child's data. No advertising. No exceptions.

**Content:** Three questions every technology must answer before entering any program:

```
1. Does it serve the child, not the corporation?
2. Does it work without an account?
3. Does it work offline, or can it?
```

If any answer is no: the technology does not enter the program.

**Visual:** The Technology Evaluation decision tree (from 07b-visual-system-pack.md, Section B2). Simple vertical tree. Green = approved. Red = rejected.

**Spoken line:** "Parents and teachers are asking Council for this. We've built the framework. Council adopting it — even as a statement of alignment — positions Frankston ahead of every other Victorian council on children's digital safety."

**What this slide does:** Converts children's digital safety from a liability into a Council asset.

**Next step visible:** "How we handle First Nations obligations."

## Slide 09 — First Nations Protocol Pathway

**Headline:** The first step is respectful process. Not content creation.

**Content (three statements):**
- Blue Snake Studio has no existing First Nations relationships in Frankston. We say so plainly.
- We follow a documented sequence: Research → Intermediary → Listening → Permission — before any cultural engagement occurs.
- No First Nations language, stories, symbols, or cultural material will appear in any project output without explicit, written, documented permission.

**Visual:** The simplified permission pathway diagram (from 06g-visual-layout-suggestions.md). Eight stages in sequence, with lock icons between each stage.

**Spoken line:** "This is how we protect the project, protect the community, and protect Council. We are not rushing this. We are doing it correctly."

**What this slide does:** Demonstrates rigour. Shows Council this risk has been thought through before they asked about it.

**Next step visible:** "What else can go wrong."

## Slide 10 — Risk Controls

**Headline:** We have mapped the risks. Here are the four that matter most.

**Content:** Four rows (highest-priority risks from the register):

| Risk | Likelihood | What We Do |
|---|---|---|
| Council withdraws support | Medium | Build school + community relationships independent of Council. Document all commitments. |
| Funding unsuccessful | Medium | Stagger applications. Pilots work at minimum viable cost. |
| Community feels excluded | Medium | Community co-design from day one. Frankston is always the subject, not the backdrop. |
| First Nations misstep | Low | Protocol pathway governs all engagement. Cultural safety advisor engaged before any contact. |

**Visual:** Simple 4-row table. Risk in column 1. Traffic light in column 2. Mitigation in column 3.

**Spoken line:** "We are not asking Council to assume risk. We are showing you the risks we have already planned for."

**What this slide does:** Demonstrates maturity. Shows Council this team has thought beyond the optimistic scenario.

**Next step visible:** "What we're specifically asking you to do."

## Slide 11 — Stage One Council Ask

**Headline:** Four asks. One small. Three easy.

**Content:**

| Ask | What It Means | What It Costs Council |
|---|---|---|
| **Partnership statement** | Name Frankston 2035 as a Council-supported initiative | Zero financial cost. Political commitment. |
| **Library pilot co-investment** | $12,000–$15,000 toward the Library Creative Pack | One line item. Matched 2:1 by BSS / grant funding. |
| **Permit facilitation** | One officer nominated to assist with site access for public art | Staff time: 2–3 hours total. |
| **Healthy Technology alignment** | A Council statement that child-facing programs are evaluated against the Charter | Zero cost. Significant reputational benefit. |

**Spoken line:** "We are not asking for a ten-year contract. We are asking for one small co-investment, a name on the project, help with paperwork, and a statement. If the pilot works — and we will prove that it works — everything else follows naturally."

**What this slide does:** Makes the ask impossible to say it's too big.

**Next step visible:** "One meeting. One decision."

## Deck Notes

**Format for presentation:** Plain slides, white background, Canela headings, Neue Haas Grotesk body. Diagrams from the Visual System Pack. No stock photography. No animation.

**Format for leaving behind:** A printed A4 booklet, saddle-stitched, with the Hero Diagram on the back cover.

**What NOT to do in the room:**
- Don't show all 12 slides sequentially if the conversation goes deep on slide 4. Follow the room.
- Don't use "innovative" or "transformative." Show, don't claim.
- Don't mention grant applications that haven't been written yet as if they're confirmed.
- Don't oversell the First Nations process — name it accurately.
