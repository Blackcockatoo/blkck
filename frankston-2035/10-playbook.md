# The Frankston Playbook
## A Practical Manual for Civic Creativity

*Version 2.0 — Expanded Scaffold*

---

### What This Is

Not a proposal. Not a strategy. Not a celebration.

A manual.

Every chapter answers one question: *how do you actually do this?*

Written for the person who has just joined the project — or who is about to start one exactly like it, somewhere else.

---

### How to Use It

Find the chapter that matches your situation. Read it. Follow the steps. Adapt what doesn't fit. Document what you change. Share it back.

Some chapters are complete because the work they describe can be reasoned from first principles. Some chapters are scaffolded because the best content will come from doing the work and writing it down honestly. The distinction is marked clearly.

---

### Chapter Format

Each chapter answers eight questions:

```
1. What this chapter is for
2. Who should read it
3. The core idea in one sentence
4. The process (step by step)
5. What you will need
6. What can go wrong
7. A real example (added as projects complete)
8. Where to go next
```

## Chapter 1 — Turning Murals into Classrooms

**For:** School principals, teachers, artists, council officers

**Core idea:** A mural is not decoration. It is a permanent piece of educational infrastructure if you design it that way from the start.

**Status:** *Scaffold. Full content written after Pilot 01 completes.*

---

**The process:**
1. Identify a school with a principal who sees art as part of learning, not separate from it
2. Select a wall — visible, durable, not facing into direct western sun
3. Define the curriculum link before briefing the artist (which year levels, which subject areas, what concepts)
4. Write the artist brief: aesthetic vision + educational function + co-design requirement
5. Run an open call or targeted approach to select an artist
6. Run the student co-design workshop (see Appendix C)
7. Finalise the concept with artist, teacher, and student sign-off
8. Execute the mural (artist leads, students observe or participate in bounded ways)
9. Install and document
10. Write the curriculum resource pack while the experience is fresh
11. Hold a community celebration — the school community, not a media event
12. Transfer ownership: the school owns the artwork and the resources

**What can go wrong:**
- Principal changes mid-project. Solution: get written commitment from the school community, not just the principal.
- Artist and teacher disagree on content. Solution: brief the artist clearly at the start that curriculum function is non-negotiable; creative execution is theirs.
- Students feel their input was performative. Solution: one student-suggested element must appear in the final artwork unchanged.

*Real example: Pilot 01 — to be documented on completion.*

## Chapter 2 — Healthy Technology

**For:** Library staff, school teachers and principals, parents, council digital policy officers

**Core idea:** A screen is a doorway or a trap. The difference is design intent. Only permit technology designed to open.

**Status:** *Written. Expand with Pilot 02 experience.*

---

### The Foundational Test

Before any technology enters a program that involves children, apply the Screen as Doorway test (see document 07c for the full concept):

> After using this technology, does the child interact more with the real world or less?

If more: the technology may be permitted.
If less: it is not permitted, regardless of educational claims made for it.

### The Three Questions

Every technology must answer yes to all three before entering a program:

**1. Does it serve the child, not the corporation?**
The product's design purpose must be the child's learning, creativity, or wellbeing — not data collection, engagement maximisation, or commercial return. Check: who built this and what did they optimise for?

**2. Does it work without an account?**
No child in a BSS program creates an account, provides an email address, or submits personal information. If the technology requires this, it does not enter the program.

**3. Does it work offline, or can it?**
Offline capability protects children from data exposure and reduces dependency on connectivity. Preference is always given to tools that function fully without a network.

### The Process: Evaluating a Technology

1. Name the proposed technology and its purpose
2. Identify who built it and what their business model is
3. Run the three questions (see Technology Evaluation diagram, 07b)
4. If all three pass: document approval in the Asset Register with date and reviewer
5. If any fail: document rejection with reason
6. If uncertain: refer to Creative Director. Do not allow while uncertain.
7. Communicate the decision to the partner organisation

### Setting Up a Healthy Tech Space in a Library

**The physical space:**
- Tables large enough for two or three children to work together — not individual carrels
- Materials visible and accessible — not locked away
- One or more analogue creative stations alongside digital ones
- Signage that explains what the space is and what it is not

**The tool selection:**
Start with what is permitted, not with what is popular.

Permitted tools for a library creative technology space (examples — evaluate each separately):
- Audio recording and mixing (local-only, no upload)
- Digital drawing and painting (offline-capable, no account)
- Stop-motion animation (local capture, no account)
- Creative writing software (offline, no account)
- Music composition tools (offline-capable)
- Physical computing (Raspberry Pi, Arduino — offline, tangible output)

Not permitted:
- Social platforms of any kind
- Platforms requiring account creation
- Streaming video (entertainment)
- Any tool that recommends content based on prior behaviour
- Any tool that stores child data on external servers without explicit, documented consent

**Training facilitators:**
A facilitator (staff or volunteer) can run the space with:
- 4-hour initial training covering: the philosophy, the tools, the protocols, what to do when something goes wrong
- One supervised session before running independently
- A one-page reference guide for the space (laminated, at the facilitator station)

The training is not about becoming a technology expert. It is about being comfortable with the tools and confident in explaining the philosophy to parents and children.

### Talking to Parents and Carers

Most parents will appreciate the straightforward explanation:

> "Everything in this space is designed so that your child creates something and then puts the device down. No accounts, no data collected, no advertising, no recommendations pulling them back in. When the session ends, it ends."

If a parent asks why a specific popular platform isn't used:

> "We evaluated it against our Healthy Technology Charter and it didn't pass. The platform's business model depends on children spending more time on it — that's the opposite of what we're doing here."

If a parent disagrees with the approach:

> "We respect that you might see it differently. This is the approach we use in this space. There are other spaces in the library where the standard platforms are available."

Do not argue. Document any feedback received.

### What Can Go Wrong

- A technology passes the three questions but turns out to create loop behaviour in practice. Solution: remove it immediately. The three questions are a filter, not a guarantee. Behaviour in practice overrides the evaluation.
- A vendor offers to "partner" by providing their platform for free. Solution: decline. Free platforms carry the same obligations to their investors as paid ones.
- A school or library partner wants to use a non-compliant tool for a specific educational reason. Solution: document the request, apply the evaluation, make the decision transparently. Do not allow exceptions under pressure.

## Chapter 3 — Community Creativity

**For:** Community development workers, arts coordinators, council community services, event organisers

**Core idea:** You do not create a creative community. You find it, show it to itself, and get out of the way.

**Status:** *Written. Expand with community engagement experience.*

---

### The First Thing to Know

Most communities are already creative. They have people who make things, tell stories, sing, draw, build, cook, and organise. They rarely have a system that connects these people to each other or to public life.

Community creativity programs succeed when they surface what already exists and give it structure. They fail when they attempt to import creativity from outside.

This means: the first step is listening. Not designing. Not programming. Listening.

### Community Mapping

Before a single event is planned or a single brief is written, spend time discovering what already exists:

**Questions to answer:**
- Who in this community is already making things? (Look beyond arts spaces — look at markets, clubs, churches, online groups, school newsletters)
- What do people make here that outsiders don't know about?
- Which organisations already support creative activity in any form?
- Where do people gather naturally? (Not venues for hire — actual gathering places)
- What has been tried before and stopped? Why?

**How to find out:**
- Walk and look
- Attend existing community events (not to recruit — to observe)
- Talk to librarians, school counsellors, community health workers — they know who makes things
- Talk to the people who have lived here longest
- Talk to recent arrivals — they often see the community more clearly

**Document the map.** Names, organisations, locations, activities. This becomes the project's network. It is also a gift to the next person.

### Open Calls That Work

An open call is an invitation to participate. Most open calls fail because they are addressed to "the creative community" — a group that does not experience itself as a group.

**A better approach:**
- Address the call to specific people or specific activities ("if you've ever..." "if you make..." "if you've thought about...")
- Tell people exactly what you're asking them to do — not a vague "involvement" but a specific contribution
- Tell them what they will receive in return (payment if applicable, credit always, time commitment honestly stated)
- Make the application frictionless — one page, not five
- Follow up personally with anyone who is close but hasn't responded

**What an open call is not:**
It is not a competition. It is not a test of commitment. It is not a way to find the most motivated people — it is a way to find the right people for this particular thing.

### Running a Co-Design Session

A co-design session is a conversation with structure. Its purpose is to produce decisions, not ideas. The difference matters.

**Before the session:**
- Know the decision you need to make. (Not "what should we do?" but "which of these three options, and why?")
- Invite people who will be affected by the decision — not people who are enthusiastic about the project
- Keep the group to 8–12 people maximum
- Prepare visual prompts, not verbal ones — show examples, show the space, show what exists
- Have a note-taker who is not the facilitator

**During the session:**
- State the decision clearly at the start
- Give people time to look and think before speaking
- Use structured turns for early contributions — not open floor discussion (which advantages louder voices)
- When an idea emerges, test it: "Who else thinks this?" Not "who disagrees?"
- Document in real time, visible to all (whiteboard, large paper)
- End with a clear statement of what was decided and what was not

**After the session:**
- Send notes to all participants within 48 hours
- Acknowledge what was not decided (and when it will be)
- Follow through on every commitment made in the room

**What can go wrong:**
- The session becomes a consultation rather than a decision. Solution: if you're not actually going to use the decision, don't hold the session.
- One person dominates. Solution: structured turns. "Let's hear from everyone who hasn't spoken yet."
- The session reaches no decision. Solution: this is information — either the decision isn't ready to be made, or the group isn't the right group for it.

### Handing Ownership to the Community

The hardest thing in community creativity work is knowing when to let go.

The signs that it is time:
- Community members are organising the next session without being asked
- The program is being talked about in spaces BSS doesn't control
- Someone outside the program is offering to resource it
- The people running it have stopped asking BSS for permission

The signs that it is not yet time:
- BSS is still the only organisation marketing the program
- Participants show up because of the relationship with BSS, not with each other
- The program has not yet survived a BSS absence

Transfer ownership formally:
- Document what exists (assets, contacts, processes)
- Introduce the incoming custodian to all key relationships
- Step back visibly and publicly
- Remain available but do not remain active

## Chapter 4 — Pilot Projects

**For:** Any organisation considering running their own pilot

**Core idea:** A pilot is only useful if it is designed to become permanent infrastructure.

**Status:** *Written from first principles. Expand with Frankston experience.*

---

### The Three Questions Every Pilot Must Answer

Before committing to a pilot, it must have honest answers to:

**1. Does it work?**
Not "did we do it" but "did it achieve what we said it would?" Define this before you start. Not after.

**2. Does it last?**
Will the outcome exist after the pilot is over, and does it exist without the pilot team continuing to maintain it?

**3. Can it be copied?**
Is the process documented well enough that someone who wasn't there can replicate it? Would they need to buy something, hire someone, or have special access to do so?

If a pilot cannot answer yes to all three, it has not proven a model — it has done a thing.

### Scoping a Pilot

A good pilot is:
- **Small enough to complete.** Scope creep kills pilots. Define the boundary of the pilot and hold it.
- **Real enough to prove something.** A demonstration project in ideal conditions with exceptional resources proves nothing about replication.
- **Designed with the exit in mind.** What is the community or institution taking over? When? How?

### Documentation as a Design Requirement

Documentation is not an afterthought. It is designed in.

For each pilot:
- Assign one person responsible for documentation (this may be the Creative Director)
- Document the process as it happens, not retrospectively
- Capture what went wrong, not just what went right
- Produce a replication guide before the pilot is considered complete
- Publish the documentation freely — under Creative Commons or equivalent

### The Handoff

Every pilot ends with a formal handoff. The handoff is not a celebration — it is a transfer of responsibility.

The handoff includes:
- A briefing document for the incoming custodian
- Introduction to all key relationships (in person where possible)
- Access to all assets (files, contacts, space, tools)
- A 90-day availability commitment from BSS for questions
- A date after which BSS is no longer responsible

### The Replication Guide

The final product of every pilot is the replication guide: a concise document that tells another organisation how to do what Frankston did.

It contains:
- What the pilot did (one paragraph)
- What it cost
- What it produced
- What made it work
- What went wrong
- What it would cost to replicate (minimum viable version)
- A checklist of prerequisites
- Contact for questions

*Real example: To be documented as each pilot completes.*

## Chapter 5 — Creative Infrastructure

**For:** Creative Directors, arts organisations, cultural policy officers

**Core idea:** The difference between a project and infrastructure is whether it is still running when the people who started it have moved on.

**Status:** *Written. Draw from Creative Operating System (document 08).*

---

### The Five Layers

From the Creative Operating System:

```
LAYER 5 — CULTURE         (visible, ephemeral — events, moments, recognition)
LAYER 4 — PROGRAMMING     (regular, sustained — workshops, walks, residencies)
LAYER 3 — SPACE           (physical and digital — where it happens)
LAYER 2 — TOOLS           (what people use to participate)
LAYER 1 — RELATIONSHIPS   (invisible, durable — trust, reciprocity, belonging)
```

Most organisations build at layers 3–5. The programs look good. The events are well-attended. The spaces are well-designed. But when the funding ends or the team changes, the whole thing collapses — because nothing was built at layers 1 and 2.

Infrastructure is built at layer 1 first, always.

### Building at Layer 1

Layer 1 is relationship. Relationships have the following properties that are relevant to this work:
- They cannot be accelerated beyond a natural pace
- They survive individual departures if they are held by the organisation, not the person
- They are the only thing that transfers ownership reliably
- They cannot be documented — they can only be introduced

**Building at layer 1 means:**
- Being present in the community before you need something from it
- Following through on every commitment, every time
- Being honest when something has gone wrong
- Returning to conversations you started
- Crediting the community visibly and permanently

### Building at Layer 2

Layer 2 is tools. Tools have the following properties:
- They outlast people
- They can be transferred
- They can be documented
- They can be evaluated against the Healthy Technology Charter

**Building at layer 2 means:**
- Choosing tools that the community can own and operate without BSS
- Documenting how the tools work and why
- Resisting the temptation to use tools that only work when BSS is present (proprietary platforms, specialist expertise)

### Asset Transfer: Designing for Community Ownership

Every asset created in this project has a planned owner at the end. This is designed in at the start.

Before any asset is created, the Creative Director asks:
- Who will own this when the pilot is over?
- Does that owner have the capacity to maintain it?
- If not, how do we build that capacity during the pilot?

Assets that cannot be transferred to a community owner should not be created.

### Documentation as Infrastructure

A project without documentation is not infrastructure — it is experience. Experience lives in people. When people leave, it goes.

Documentation that functions as infrastructure:
- Is written for someone who was not present
- Describes the reasoning behind decisions, not just the decisions
- Includes what went wrong
- Is kept current (not produced at the end and filed)
- Is held in a publicly accessible location

### The Handoff as a Creative Act

The handoff — the moment when BSS formally transfers responsibility to the community or institution — is not an administrative event. It is a creative act. It deserves the same care as the project it closes.

A well-designed handoff:
- Celebrates what was built
- Names everyone who contributed
- Introduces the new custodians publicly
- Provides everything the custodians need to continue without asking
- Leaves a visible record that BSS was there — and then left

The leaving is part of the work.

```
Session purpose: [Specific decision to be made]
Date / time / location: [Details]
Participants: [Number, roles — do not name individuals in template]
Facilitator: [Name]
Note-taker: [Name — not the facilitator]

PRE-SESSION PREPARATION
□  Confirm participants 48 hours ahead
□  Prepare visual prompts (examples, images, space photos)
□  Set up room: tables for groups of 3–4, materials available
□  Prepare structured prompt cards for the opening activity

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SESSION RUN SHEET

0:00 — WELCOME (5 min)
Facilitator introduces purpose: "Today we need to decide [specific thing]."
Name the constraints: what is decided already, what is open.
Name the process: "We'll work in three stages."

0:05 — INDIVIDUAL REFLECTION (10 min)
Each participant responds to 2–3 written prompts (cards provided).
No discussion yet. Writing or drawing, not speaking.
Prompt examples: "What do you already love about this space?"
"What one thing would make this work for you?"
"What concerns you about this proposal?"

0:15 — SMALL GROUP DISCUSSION (20 min)
Groups of 3–4 share their reflections.
Each group identifies their top 2 points of agreement and 1 point of tension.
Note-taker circulates and captures on large paper.

0:35 — FULL GROUP (25 min)
Each group reports: agreement points first, tension second.
Facilitator maps points on large paper visible to all.
Look for convergence. Test it: "Does anyone strongly disagree with this?"
Name what is emerging as the decision.

1:00 — DECISION POINT (15 min)
Facilitator names the decision as it has emerged.
Confirm: "Have I represented this correctly?"
Note any dissent: document it, do not suppress it.
Confirm: who acts on this decision, by when.

1:15 — CLOSE (15 min)
Thank participants.
State what happens next: when notes will be shared, when the decision is enacted.
Confirm that participants will be credited for their contribution.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

POST-SESSION
□  Notes written up within 24 hours
□  Notes shared to all participants for accuracy check
□  Decision documented in project register
□  Follow-through confirmed in next project update
```

### Appendix F — Glossary

**Acknowledgement of Country**
A formal statement recognising the Traditional Custodians of the land on which an event or meeting takes place. Appropriate and necessary. Not a substitute for engagement.

**Asset Register**
The live record of all project assets — intellectual, digital, physical, and relational. Kept current throughout the project.

**Co-design**
A process in which the people affected by a decision participate in making it. Not consultation (asking after the fact). Not surveys (asking without conversation). A structured, facilitated process that produces decisions, not just opinions.

**Constitution**
The foundational document of Frankston 2035. It governs all other documents. It is the source of truth when any document conflict arises.

**Country**
As used in Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Australian contexts: a living relationship between people, place, water, sky, animals, plants, and story. Not simply land.

**Creative Director**
Blue Snake Studio's designated decision-maker for the Frankston 2035 project. Responsible for quality, coherence, and protocol compliance.

**Creative Infrastructure**
The systems, relationships, spaces, and tools that allow creative activity to persist over time, independent of any single person or organisation.

**Cultural safety**
The condition in which all people — particularly First Nations people — can participate in a program, space, or public work without being diminished, misrepresented, or disrespected. Cultural safety is designed in, not added later.

**Healthy Technology Charter**
The Frankston 2035 document that defines which technologies may and may not be used in programs involving children. Governed by the three questions.

**Horizon**
One of three phases of the Frankston 2035 roadmap: Prove (2026–2027), Build (2028–2030), Replicate (2031–2035).

**Pilot**
A time-bounded project designed to prove a model and produce permanent infrastructure. A pilot is not a trial run — it produces a real, lasting outcome.

**Playbook**
This document. A practical manual for doing the work.

**RAP — Registered Aboriginal Party**
A First Nations organisation formally recognised under the Aboriginal Heritage Act 2006 (Vic) as having responsibility for protecting and managing cultural heritage in a defined area. The Bunurong Land Council Aboriginal Corporation is the RAP for the Frankston area.

**Replication guide**
The document produced at the end of each pilot that describes how another organisation could do the same thing. Produced before a pilot is considered complete.

**Screen as Doorway**
The conceptual framework for Healthy Technology in Frankston 2035. A screen is a doorway when it pushes the child into the world. It is a trap when it pulls the child in. Only doorways are permitted. (See document 07c.)

## A Note on This Playbook

Chapters 2, 3, 4, and 5 are written from first principles. They can be used now.

Chapters 1, 6, 7, 8, 9, and 10 are scaffolded. The headings are real, the sequence is real, but the content must come from the experience of doing the work.

A playbook written before the work happens is fiction. A playbook written from the work is instruction.

This document will be ready when the pilots are done, the relationships are real, and the people who did the work have written it down honestly.
