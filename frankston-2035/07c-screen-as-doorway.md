# Screen as Doorway
## A Concept and Diagram for Frankston 2035

---

### The Idea

A screen is not a destination. It is a door.

There are two kinds of technology that put a screen in front of a child. One kind uses the screen to pull the child in — deeper and deeper into the platform, the feed, the loop — until the child is no longer a person in the world but a user generating data. This kind of technology is optimised for captivity.

The other kind uses the screen to push the child out — into a story, a place, a person, a question, a walk, a creation. This kind of technology is optimised for encounter. The screen opens. You pass through it. The experience exists in the world, not in the device. The screen closes.

Frankston 2035 uses only the second kind.

The screen is the door. The world is the destination. The technology has done its job when you forget it was there.

**Layout:** Three zones, horizontal, left to right.

```
│ WITH PHONE │                    │                │
│            │  [QR code scanned] │ story opens    │

│            │  [audio plays]     │ question asked │
│            │                    │ artwork found  │
│            │  ←   door   →      │ memory made    │
```

**The screen is drawn as a literal open doorway** — a rectangle with no fill, set within the layout. The child stands to the left of the doorway. The world extends to the right.

The doorway is transparent. The child walks through it. On the other side: a mural, a story, a QR walk stop, a creative tool.

**Two contrasting panels** beneath the main diagram:

Left panel (red-tinted): `TECHNOLOGY THAT TRAPS`
- Screen as mirror — reflects the child back at themselves
- Loop arrows cycling inside the screen
- Child appears smaller/dimmer on the inside
- Label: "Engagement maximised. World shrinks."

Right panel (green-tinted): `TECHNOLOGY THAT OPENS`
- Screen as doorway — passes through, opens
- Arrow pointing outward into the world
- Child appears larger/brighter on the outside
- Label: "Experience complete. World expands."

### The Three Tests

These three questions translate the diagram into a practical evaluation:

**Test 1: After using this technology, does the child interact more with the real world or less?**

More: the technology opened a door.
Less: the technology closed one.

**Test 2: Does the experience end?**

A screen-as-doorway experience has a natural end point. The walk finishes. The story concludes. The tool produces something. The child puts the phone down.
Technology optimised for captivity never ends. The next video autoplays. The next level opens. The feed refreshes.

**Test 3: Who benefits when the child uses it more?**

If the child benefits: it may be a doorway.
If the platform benefits: it is almost certainly a trap.

### Application to Each Pilot

**Pilot 01 — Living Mural:**
The screen is not present. The mural is the experience. If a QR code is added to the mural as an optional layer, it opens a story and closes. The experience ends at the wall.

**Pilot 02 — Healthy Tech Library:**
Every tool in the Healthy Tech Library is evaluated against the Screen as Doorway test. Tools that create loop behaviours — open-ended games, social feeds, recommendation engines — do not pass. Tools that produce a finished thing — a drawing, a composition, a story — do.

**Pilot 03 — QR Art Walk:**
The phone is used to open a story or an artwork. The story ends. The audio finishes. The walk continues. The child looks up from the phone and sees the place the content described. The screen served its purpose and was put away.

### Language for Public Use

When explaining Healthy Technology to parents, teachers, or community members, use this language rather than the technical framework:

> "We ask one question about technology near children: does it push them into the world, or pull them out of it? If it pushes them out, we use it. If it pulls them in, we don't."

This is the Screen as Doorway concept in one sentence. It is more useful than a charter in most conversations.

---

### What This Concept Is Not

This concept is not anti-technology. It is not anti-screen. It is not a nostalgic argument for analogue experience.

It is a specific claim about design intent: technology designed to maximise engagement time is structurally incompatible with children's flourishing. Technology designed to produce a complete experience and then end is compatible.

The distinction is design, not medium. A physical book can trap a child in a loop. A screen can open a door into the world. The question is always: who designed this, and what did they optimise for?
